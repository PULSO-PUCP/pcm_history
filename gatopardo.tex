\title{The Instability Itself Is the Institution: Ministerial 
Survival and Institutional Learning in Peru, 1821--2026}

Instituto PULSO, Pontificia Universidad Cat\'olica del Per\'u}

\abstract{Why do political systems that change so much change so 
little in how they govern? This paper addresses that question 
through an original dataset of 1,062 ministerial spells covering 
five portfolios in Peru from 1821 to 2026. Rather than treating 
ministerial duration as the outcome to be explained, we treat it 
as a signal: an observable trace of the continuity conditions 
under which governing experience either accumulates into 
institutional knowledge or is repeatedly lost. We organize the 
analysis around two analytical heuristics. The first is the 
institutional epoch --- the path-dependent period between 
critical junctures --- which captures whether successive 
historical ruptures produced durable improvements in continuity 
conditions. The second is the protective belt --- a localized 
architecture of external accountability relationships that, where 
it exists, insulates certain ministries from the political logic 
governing the rest of the cabinet. Kaplan--Meier survival curves, 
log-rank tests, and Cox proportional hazards models show that 
epochs explain ministerial survival far better than individual 
traits or portfolio membership, and that continuity conditions 
did not improve monotonically across two centuries. The largest 
shift in the data occurred between the Oligarchic State and the 
Power-Sharing epoch; the most recent democratic period represents 
a partial regression toward the founding pattern of chronic 
interruption. The protective belt emerges only after 1993, when 
external accountability structures raised the cost of Economy 
minister replacement beyond what domestic political pressure 
alone could sustain. The central theoretical finding is that 
chronic ministerial instability in Peru is not evidence of absent 
institutions but of a durable informal one: the escape valve 
through which political pressure is absorbed without producing 
structural change. The instability itself is the institution.}

\section*{Introduction}
% ============================================================

Governing is hard. Education, health, and public safety are not technical
problems with known solutions --- they are complex social challenges that
resist quick fixes and demand sustained institutional effort. Good policy
takes time to design, time to implement, and more time still to reach the
people it is meant to serve. Good practices percolate gradually. Organizations learn slowly.  Citizens feel change only after years of consistent effort in the same direction. In the big picture, where governance improved, it did so through institutional correction after crisis --- converting rupture into consolidation, learning from failure rather than repeating it.

After more than two hundred years of republican life, the Peruvian political class has repeatedly changed governments, cabinets, constitutions, and political coalitions. Yet the underlying pattern remains remarkably stable. Each new administration appoints a cabinet whose members understand, implicitly or explicitly, the terms of their engagement: they are expected to manage complexity on behalf of the leader, but they may also be sacrificed whenever political pressure demands a visible response. Their departure rarely resolves the conflict that triggered it. The resignation of a premier or any other minister may relieve immediate pressure, but it seldom addresses the underlying causes of crisis. Cabinet reshuffles signal responsiveness without necessarily producing institutional correction.

This persistence should not be mistaken for an absence of learning. Political actors adapt continuously. Over time, they have become increasingly adept at navigating constitutional ambiguities, managing blame, and deploying ministerial replacement as a mechanism for absorbing public dissatisfaction. If the political class has learned anything, it has been how to reproduce the system's response to crisis rather than how to transform it. Governments change. Ministers change. The repertoire through which instability is managed remains largely intact. This cycle has marked much of Peru's republican history.



This vicious circle is generally called path dependence --- a sophisticated way to say that the inertia of accumulated choices makes deviation increasingly costly and increasingly unlikely. And yet paths do break. Oxford philosopher Roman \textcite{krznaric_how_2024} argues that radical change requires what he calls the disruption nexus: a crisis, disruptive movements that amplify it, and powerful ideas that are ready to fill the space the crisis opens. France's Fifth Republic had all three: the Algerian War as crisis, De~Gaulle's movement as disruptive force, and a constitutional design --- the strong executive --- as the idea ready to be deployed. It was not one event that broke France's path. It was a multigenerational accumulation of chronic instability that the Third and Fourth Republics never resolved. The Fifth Republic became the enduring legacy of a critical juncture in French history. More importantly, it appears to have altered the conditions under which political continuity was produced. The result was not merely institutional change, but a durable shift in the patterns of executive stability that had characterized French politics for more than a century.



France, before the Fifth Republic, was not very different from Peru during the previous hundred years. Both experienced remarkably high levels of executive instability, reflected in similar numbers of premier appointments per decade. Figure~\ref{fig:premiers} tells the story visually. Peru peaked at 23 appointments in the 1890s; France reached 20 in the 1930s. Yet the advent of the Fifth Republic in 1958 initiated sharply diverging trajectories. French premier appointments fell to a stable band of three to six per decade and have remained there ever since, while Peru is returning to levels observed in the nineteenth century. Peru has experienced no shortage of potential disruption nexuses --- or, as we will later call them, critical junctures: independence without state-building, the War of the Pacific, hyperinflation, terrorism, the fall of Fujimori, and a permanent constitutional crisis since 2016. Each represented a moment in which alternative institutional trajectories became plausible.


The question, however, is not whether institutional change occurred. Peru's republican history is full of reforms, constitutions, regime transitions, and political ruptures. The question is whether those ruptures altered the conditions under which political experience could be preserved, accumulated, and transmitted over time. From this perspective, a critical juncture is important not simply because it produces a new institutional arrangement, but because it may establish a political legacy capable of reshaping the broader political framework within which governments operate. France appears to have experienced such a transformation after 1958. Peru's trajectory raises the possibility that institutional innovation and the accumulation of political experience do not necessarily coincide.

This paper approaches that question through ministerial duration. Ministerial survival does not measure political experience directly. Rather, it provides observable evidence about the continuity under which political experience may accumulate. Political science has traditionally examined these issues through separate research traditions. Ministerial duration has been studied using survival and event-history analysis, whereas long-term political development has been examined through the comparative-historical literature on critical junctures and path dependence. This paper brings these traditions together by using ministerial duration to evaluate whether successive political frameworks generated different opportunities for the accumulation of political experience.

The analysis is organized around two complementary heuristics. The first compares patterns of ministerial continuity across historically distinct political frameworks in order to examine how opportunities for knowledge accumulation varied over time. The second compares ministerial continuity across portfolios operating within the same political framework, recognizing that some governmental functions may be institutionally protected from the broader dynamics affecting the rest of the cabinet. Together, these two perspectives distinguish historical variation from organizational variation in the conditions under which political experience accumulates.

The question guiding this study is straightforward: \emph{Have Peru's successive political frameworks generated increasingly favorable opportunities for the accumulation of political experience?}

This question cannot be answered directly. Knowledge accumulation is not directly observable in historical data. Instead, the study approaches the problem indirectly through patterns of ministerial duration. If continuity provides the necessary condition for knowledge accumulation, then systematic differences in ministerial continuity should reveal differences in the opportunities under which political experience accumulates.

The theoretical framework translates this general question into three empirical questions. \emph{First}, what continuity conditions characterized each political framework? \emph{Second}, did those continuity conditions differ systematically across successive political frameworks? \emph{Third}, within the same political framework, do some ministerial portfolios systematically exhibit greater continuity than others?

To answer these questions, we construct an original dataset of individual ministerial spells covering the period from 1821 to 2026. The dataset includes the portfolios most relevant to the argument developed here: the premier, who coordinates the cabinet and sustains the political viability of the executive; the economy minister, whose decisions shape the state's fiscal and investment capacity; and the ministers of Interior, Health, and Education, whose portfolios address some of the country's most persistent public challenges according to official yearly surveys \parencite{inei_encuesta_2024}. The ministerial spells are subsequently organized into historically distinct political frameworks derived from Peru's critical junctures \parencite{lopez_jimenez_peru_2025}. Combining descriptive analysis with survival models, the study evaluates how opportunities for knowledge accumulation varied across political frameworks and across ministerial portfolios.

\subsection{Trait-Based Explanations of Ministerial Survival}

Trait-based approaches explain ministerial survival through characteristics of the officeholder. Across different institutional settings, scholars have shown that ministers' professional backgrounds, political careers, partisan affiliations, and personal resources influence their probability of remaining in office. Early studies emphasized broad distinctions between managerial and parliamentary profiles, as well as the effects of career trajectories and prior political experience \parencite{blondel_ministerial_1988}. Subsequent work expanded the range of relevant characteristics to include age, education, gender, portfolio rank, and seniority within government \parencite{fischer_duration_2012,dewan_corrective_2005}.

More recent research has refined these categories by focusing on the organizational and political resources that ministers bring to office. Feliú Ribeiro and López Burian show that technical expertise and membership in presidential networks increase ministerial survival, while prior electoral experience may paradoxically operate as a risk factor \parencite{feliu_ribeiro_foreign_2023}. Across four Latin American countries between 1945 and 2020, career politicians proved more vulnerable to dismissal than technocrats. The finding suggests that ministerial value cannot be reduced to political experience alone; survival depends on the type of resources that political leaders seek to preserve.

The literature has also moved beyond simple dichotomies between partisan and non-partisan ministers. Camerlo and Castaldo argue that partisan affiliation conceals substantial variation in the strength of a minister's relationship with the party organization. Their framework distinguishes among leaders, high-ranks, low-ranks, newcomers, shadows, and defectors according to levels of engagement, consolidation, and hierarchical position within the party \parencite{camerlo_government_2024}. Applied to the Argentine cabinet under Alberto Fernández, this approach reveals that apparently strong partisan cabinets may actually consist of ministers with limited organizational leverage over their parties \parencite{camerlo_poderes_2024}. For survival analysis, the implication is straightforward: ministers classified under the same partisan label may possess very different capacities to resist political pressure.

Taken together, these studies demonstrate that ministerial duration is shaped by who ministers are. Technical expertise, organizational embeddedness, political experience, and personal resources all influence survival prospects. Yet the significance of these traits depends on the environments in which ministers operate. The same characteristic may be rewarded in one institutional setting and penalized in another. This observation leads to the second major family of explanations.

\subsection{Context-Based Explanations of Ministerial Survival}

Context-based approaches explain ministerial survival through characteristics of the political environment rather than characteristics of individual ministers. In this perspective, ministers survive or fail because of the institutional and political constraints surrounding them.

The comparative literature consistently identifies coalition dynamics as a central determinant of ministerial duration. Coalition type, minority government status, electoral volatility, and party-system fragmentation all shape the likelihood of ministerial replacement independently of individual characteristics \parencite{huber_replacing_2008}. Similarly, political crises, scandals, protests, electoral calendars, and term limits alter the incentives presidents face when deciding whether to retain or dismiss ministers \parencite{camerlo_minister_2015,alexiadou_commitment_2019}. Even macroeconomic performance influences survival probabilities by modifying the broader political environment within which governments operate \parencite{feliu_ribeiro_foreign_2023}.

The case literature illustrates these mechanisms particularly clearly. For Colombia, Mejía Guinand et al.\ find that individual characteristics matter, but the institutional configuration of the political system ultimately determines the range within which those characteristics can operate \parencite{mejia_es_2021}. Constitutional reforms, changes in party-system structure, and coalition arrangements alter the political logic governing ministerial careers. A similar conclusion emerges from Chile. Avendaño and Dávila show that ministerial turnover during the Concertación governments was driven primarily by coalition management considerations rather than by individual performance. Finance ministers consistently exhibited the longest tenures regardless of personal characteristics \parencite{avendano_rotacion_2012}.

Recent work has also refined the contextual analysis of ministerial portfolios. Portfolio importance has traditionally been measured through expert rankings that assign ministries a single level of salience. Camerlo and Martínez-Gallardo argue that such measures obscure important differences among ministries, particularly in contexts characterized by weak party institutionalization \parencite{camerlo_por_2022}. Their framework decomposes ministerial value into policy-management capacity, discretional resource allocation, political agenda influence, and inter-portfolio coordination authority. Different political environments activate different dimensions of portfolio value, producing distinct survival incentives for presidents and ministers alike.

Taken together, these studies show that ministerial survival depends not only on who ministers are but also on where they operate. Institutional structures, coalition arrangements, political crises, economic conditions, and portfolio characteristics shape the opportunities and constraints governing ministerial careers.

\subsection{Reinterpreting Ministerial Duration}

The two families of explanations are not competing research programs. Virtually every study combines individual and contextual covariates, with political environments conditioning the effects of ministerial traits. The resulting literature has generated a rich understanding of why some ministers survive longer than others.

What the literature has not asked is a prior question: what do patterns of ministerial survival reveal about the conditions under which political experience is collectively preserved, accumulated, and transmitted over time? Existing studies treat ministerial duration as the outcome to be explained. This paper instead treats ministerial duration as evidence about continuity. Rather than asking why one minister survives longer than another, it asks what patterns of ministerial continuity reveal about the political environments within which knowledge may accumulate.

The next section develops a deductive theoretical framework. It begins by establishing continuity as the necessary condition for knowledge accumulation. It then explains why opportunities for knowledge accumulation vary across political frameworks and why continuity may also vary across ministerial portfolios operating within the same political framework. Together, these propositions provide the conceptual foundation for interpreting ministerial duration as evidence about the historical and organizational conditions under which political experience accumulates.



\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:theoretical_framework}


The theoretical framework developed in this paper is organized deductively. It begins by establishing an axiom defining the conditions under which political knowledge may accumulate. From this axiom, two propositions specify the historical and organizational contexts in which continuity conditions vary. Proposition~1 explains variation in continuity conditions across institutional epochs, while Proposition~2 explains variation in continuity conditions across ministerial portfolios within institutional epochs. Together, the axiom and propositions establish the conceptual vocabulary, logical structure, and empirical interpretation used throughout the remainder of the paper.

Throughout the discussion, arrows ($\rightarrow$) are used as heuristic representations of hypothesized causal influence. They indicate a proposed direction of influence rather than a specific sign, magnitude, or deterministic effect.


\subsection{Axiom A1: Knowledge Accumulation Requires Continuity}

The literature reviewed above treats ministerial survival as the outcome to be explained. Most studies seek to account for variation in survival across individual appointments by focusing on ministers' personal characteristics, political context, or the interaction between the two. This paper adopts a different perspective. Rather than explaining why some ministers survive longer than others, it asks what patterns of ministerial survival reveal about the continuity conditions characterizing a particular institutional order.

The starting point is the observation that ministerial duration is not politically important in itself. What duration provides is time. Time allows experience to be retained, shared, and embedded within organizations rather than repeatedly lost through replacement. From this perspective, ministerial survival becomes relevant because it provides information about continuity.

Continuity, in turn, points toward a broader question: whether political systems create conditions under which experience can accumulate and be transmitted over time. A central insight of organizational learning theory is that institutions benefit from experience only when that experience can be retained and incorporated into future action \parencite{levitt_organizational_1988}. Continuity is therefore a necessary condition for processes through which knowledge may accumulate within organizations.

For the purposes of this study, the central concept is \textbf{knowledge accumulation}, understood as the retention, articulation, and transmission of experience within organizations. Ministerial duration is not treated as a direct measure of knowledge accumulation itself. Rather, it is interpreted as an observable indicator of the continuity conditions under which knowledge accumulation becomes more or less likely.

The deductive structure of the theoretical framework begins with Axiom~A1:

\begin{equation}
\tag{A1}
\texttt{Continuity}
\rightarrow
\texttt{Knowledge\ Accumulation}
\label{ax:continuity}
\end{equation}

Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity} states that political knowledge can accumulate only under conditions of continuity. Continuity does not guarantee that knowledge will accumulate, nor does it determine the quality of the knowledge ultimately produced. Rather, it establishes the necessary organizational condition under which experience may be retained, articulated, transmitted, and eventually embedded within institutional routines. Without continuity, opportunities for knowledge accumulation are repeatedly interrupted before experience can become embedded within organizational practice.


Having established continuity as the necessary condition for knowledge accumulation, the next theoretical task is to explain why this relationship holds. Why should continuity matter for the accumulation of political knowledge? The literature provides two complementary answers. The first emerges from organizational theories of knowledge management, which explain how organizations create, retain, and transmit knowledge. The second emerges from theories of public policy and political decision-making, which explain why effective governance itself requires continuity over time. Although these traditions developed largely independently, both converge on the same conclusion embodied in Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity}.

The first tradition is represented by the organizational learning literature. Nonaka's theory of organizational knowledge creation conceives knowledge creation as a cumulative process through which tacit knowledge becomes explicit, is shared within the organization, and is eventually internalized into routines and practices \parencite{nonaka_knowledge-creating_1998}. New knowledge emerges through repeated interaction among organizational members, the articulation of experience into formal procedures, and the gradual incorporation of those procedures into everyday organizational behavior. This process cannot be compressed into a single decision, nor can it survive repeated interruptions without loss. Continuity therefore provides the temporal condition under which organizational knowledge can progressively accumulate.

Cohen, March, and Olsen reinforce this conclusion from a different perspective. Their theory of \emph{organized anarchy} characterizes organizations by problematic preferences, unclear technology, and \emph{fluid participation} \parencite{cohen_garbage_1972}. Applied to cabinet politics, fluid participation resembles the absence of continuity: decision-makers are replaced before they can develop a shared understanding of the problems they were appointed to address. Under such conditions, the organizational environment required for retaining, articulating, and transmitting experience becomes difficult to sustain. The organizational learning tradition therefore converges on the conclusion that continuity is a prerequisite for sustained knowledge accumulation.

A second line of support emerges from theories of public policy and political decision-making. Simon's theory of bounded rationality argues that complex policy challenges initially present themselves as \emph{ill-structured problems} rather than as clearly defined decision tasks. Because decision-makers operate under conditions of bounded rationality, effective problem solving requires a sequential process in which problems are progressively structured through the identification of relevant constraints, the decomposition of complexity into manageable subproblems, and the gradual refinement of alternative courses of action \parencite[4--5]{foss_simon_2005}. Each stage of problem solving provides the foundation upon which subsequent stages can proceed, making organizational progress dependent on sustained engagement over time rather than isolated decisions \parencite[12--17]{foss_simon_2005}. Premature ministerial replacement interrupts this cumulative process before problems become sufficiently structured for effective organizational action.

Lindblom reaches a similar conclusion from the perspective of incremental policymaking. Because policymakers cannot fully specify objectives, evaluate every alternative, or anticipate all consequences, policy develops through successive limited comparisons that build upon previous decisions rather than through comprehensive optimization \parencite{lindblom_science_1959}. Governing therefore depends on the cumulative experience generated through repeated rounds of adjustment, making continuity a prerequisite for coherent policy development.

Finally, Allison's three conceptual models independently reinforce Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity}. Under the rational actor model, continuity allows decision-makers to accumulate information and refine choices under uncertainty. Under the organizational process model, continuity enables experience to become embedded in routines and standard operating procedures \parencite{allison_conceptual_1969}. Under the governmental politics model, continuity preserves the bargaining relationships, political capital, and mutual expectations required for coordinated action among political actors. Although these three models were developed to explain governmental decision-making from different analytical perspectives, both identify continuity as the organizational condition under which experience becomes cumulative rather than episodic.

Taken together, these two theoretical traditions converge on the same foundational conclusion despite approaching the problem from different directions. The organizational learning literature explains how knowledge is created, retained, and transmitted within organizations. The public policy literature explains why effective governing requires sustained engagement with complex problems, incremental adjustment, organizational routines, and durable political relationships. Although developed independently, both traditions ultimately support the same foundational principle embodied in Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity}: knowledge accumulation requires continuity.



\subsection{Proposition 1: Political Frameworks Shape Opportunities for Knowledge Accumulation}

Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity} established that knowledge accumulation requires continuity. Proposition~1 addresses a different question. If continuity enables knowledge accumulation, why should opportunities for knowledge accumulation differ across history? More specifically, why should one political framework generate different opportunities for knowledge accumulation than another? Answering this question requires moving beyond organizational processes to the broader political environments within which governments operate.

The central concept proposed here is the \textbf{political framework}. A political framework refers to a configuration of political institutions, governing arrangements, and actor relationships that structures political action during a particular historical period. Political frameworks define the broader political environment within which governments operate. Because successive political frameworks need not reproduce the same political conditions, they may also generate different opportunities for knowledge accumulation.

The concept of political framework is grounded in the Critical Juncture tradition of comparative-historical analysis. Rather than viewing political development as a continuous sequence of incremental change, this tradition conceives history as a succession of distinct political orders separated by episodes of rapid institutional transformation---critical junctures. Critical junctures generate enduring political legacies that persist through path-dependent reproduction. These legacies define historically distinct political frameworks within which political actors govern until a subsequent critical juncture establishes a different historical legacy \parencite{capoccia_study_2007,collier_critical_2022}.

\begin{equation}
\tag{P1}
\texttt{Political\ Framework}
\rightarrow
\texttt{Knowledge\ Accumulation}
\label{prop:framework_ka}
\end{equation}

Proposition~\ref{prop:framework_ka} states that political frameworks shape opportunities for knowledge accumulation. The proposition does not modify Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity}; rather, it specifies the historical context within which the axiom operates. Because knowledge accumulation requires continuity, differences between successive political frameworks become theoretically important insofar as they facilitate, constrain, or repeatedly interrupt the continuity conditions that make knowledge accumulation possible.


An important implication follows. Institutional innovation should not automatically be interpreted as an improvement in opportunities for knowledge accumulation. Political systems may enact new constitutions, administrative reforms, or governing arrangements without fundamentally altering the conditions under which organizations accumulate experience. Conversely, successive political frameworks that differ substantially in their formal institutional architecture may nevertheless generate similar opportunities for knowledge accumulation. The relevant theoretical question is therefore not whether institutions change, but whether transitions from one political framework to another systematically alter the opportunities for knowledge accumulation.

This proposition provides the basis for defining \textbf{institutional learning}. Institutional learning is conceptualized here as a historical property rather than an organizational one. Because knowledge accumulation cannot be observed directly across political frameworks, institutional learning is inferred from historical differences in the opportunities for knowledge accumulation generated by successive political frameworks. Proposition~\ref{prop:framework_ka} therefore establishes the historical dimension of the theoretical framework by linking transitions between political frameworks to changes in opportunities for knowledge accumulation.




\subsection{Proposition 2: Protective Belts Preserve Continuity Within Political Frameworks}

Proposition~1 established that opportunities for knowledge accumulation vary across political frameworks. Proposition~2 addresses a different source of variation. Even within the same political framework, continuity is not necessarily distributed uniformly across ministerial portfolios. Some ministries exhibit greater continuity than others despite operating under the same historical and political environment. Explaining this within-framework variation requires an additional organizational mechanism.

The concept proposed here is the \textbf{protective belt}\footnote{The term ``protective belt'' is borrowed from Lakatos's philosophy of science, where it refers to the set of auxiliary assumptions that shields the hard core of a research programme from immediate falsification \parencite{lakatos_methodology_1978}. The concept is used here metaphorically to denote institutional arrangements that shield ministerial continuity from political pressures.}. A protective belt consists of institutional arrangements that increase the political cost of ministerial replacement, thereby making continuity more likely within a particular ministerial portfolio.

\begin{equation}
\tag{P2}
\texttt{Protective\ Belt}
\rightarrow
\texttt{Continuity}
\label{prop:protective_belt}
\end{equation}

Proposition~\ref{prop:protective_belt} complements rather than modifies Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity}. Whereas Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity} establishes continuity as the necessary condition for knowledge accumulation, Proposition~2 explains why continuity itself may vary across organizations operating under the same political framework. Protective belts therefore provide a localized organizational mechanism through which continuity can be preserved despite the broader political environment.

Comparative evidence suggests that such protective arrangements emerge under particular patterns of political accountability. Feliú Ribeiro and López Burian show that foreign affairs portfolios survive significantly longer than domestic portfolios across Latin America \parencite{feliu_ribeiro_foreign_2023}. They argue that foreign ministers become accountable not only to domestic political actors but also to international audiences, increasing the political costs of replacement and thereby making continuity more likely. Their findings illustrate how portfolio-specific institutional arrangements can produce different continuity patterns within the same political framework.

Protective belts are not expected to remain attached to the same ministerial portfolios over time. Because critical junctures reshape political legacies and redefine the priorities of successive political frameworks, the portfolios requiring greater continuity may also change. Protective belts therefore constitute organizational adaptations operating within political frameworks rather than permanent characteristics of particular ministries.

Proposition~\ref{prop:protective_belt} establishes the organizational dimension of the theoretical framework. Whereas Proposition~1 explains why opportunities for knowledge accumulation vary across political frameworks, Proposition~2 explains why continuity varies across ministerial portfolios operating within the same political framework. Together, the two propositions specify the historical and organizational contexts within which Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity} operates.

\section{Research Design and Analytical Strategy}\label{sec:research_design}

The theoretical framework established three complementary statements. Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity} identifies continuity as the necessary organizational condition for knowledge accumulation. Proposition~\ref{prop:framework_ka} explains why opportunities for knowledge accumulation may vary across political frameworks, while Proposition~\ref{prop:protective_belt} explains why continuity may also vary across ministerial portfolios operating within the same political framework.

The purpose of the research design is not to observe these theoretical constructs directly. Knowledge accumulation, political frameworks, and protective belts are latent concepts rather than observable variables. Instead, the research design derives observable implications from the theoretical framework and specifies how empirical patterns should be interpreted. Ministerial duration constitutes the principal observable quantity throughout the analysis and serves as the empirical basis from which continuity is inferred.

The analytical strategy therefore proceeds in three stages. First, the theoretical concepts are operationalized heuristically. Second, the observable implications of Propositions~\ref{prop:framework_ka} and~\ref{prop:protective_belt} are evaluated statistically. Third, the resulting empirical patterns are interpreted using historically grounded heuristics derived from the Critical Juncture tradition.

\subsection{Heuristic Operationalization}

The theoretical concepts introduced in the previous section cannot be measured directly. Rather than treating them as directly observable variables, this study establishes theoretically justified correspondences between abstract concepts and empirical observations. These heuristic operationalizations do not claim to measure the theoretical constructs themselves; instead, they provide the interpretive bridge through which the propositions can be evaluated empirically.


\subsubsection{Political Frameworks}

Political frameworks constitute the historical units through which Proposition~\ref{prop:framework_ka} is evaluated. Because political frameworks are theoretical constructs rather than directly observable entities, they require heuristic operationalization.

Following the Critical Juncture tradition of comparative-historical analysis, political frameworks are operationalized through the enduring political legacies generated by completed critical junctures. Rather than partitioning history according to arbitrary chronological periods, presidential administrations, or constitutional reforms, this operationalization identifies relatively stable political environments bounded by critical junctures whose legacies redefine the conditions under which political actors govern.

\subsubsection{Continuity}

Continuity constitutes the central observable implication of the theoretical framework. It is not measured directly. Instead, continuity is inferred heuristically from ministerial duration.

Following Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity}, longer ministerial spells indicate greater opportunities for organizational continuity, whereas shorter spells indicate more frequent interruption of governing experience. Ministerial duration is therefore interpreted neither as a measure of ministerial quality, political effectiveness, nor policy success, but as evidence concerning the continuity conditions under which knowledge accumulation becomes more or less feasible.

This interpretation does not imply that continuity is the only determinant of ministerial duration. Individual characteristics, political crises, presidential strategies, coalition dynamics, and other contextual factors may also influence ministerial survival. Rather, Axiom~\ref{ax:continuity} identifies continuity as the theoretical dimension of ministerial duration that is relevant for evaluating the propositions developed in this paper.


\subsubsection{Protective Belts}

Protective belts likewise remain unobservable. Their existence is inferred indirectly from systematic differences in continuity across ministerial portfolios operating within the same political framework.

Observed differences in continuity do not constitute direct evidence of protective belts. Rather, they represent empirical patterns that are consistent with the existence of institutional arrangements capable of shielding particular portfolios from the political pressures affecting ministerial turnover.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Analytical Strategy}

The empirical implications of the propositions are evaluated through two complementary comparisons.

The first comparison evaluates Proposition~\ref{prop:framework_ka}. Because political frameworks represent successive historical environments, the relevant empirical implication concerns changes in continuity associated with transitions between political frameworks,

\[
\Delta PF_{i,i+1}
\longrightarrow
\Delta C_{i,i+1}.
\]

where $\Delta PF_{i,i+1}$ denotes the transition between two successive political frameworks, and $\Delta C_{i,i+1}$ denotes the corresponding change in continuity inferred from ministerial duration.

The second comparison evaluates Proposition~\ref{prop:protective_belt}. Because protective belts operate within political frameworks, the relevant empirical implication concerns systematic differences in continuity across ministerial portfolios within the same political framework:

[
\exists PB_{k}^{(f)}
\longrightarrow
\Delta C_{k}^{(f)} \neq 0
]

where (PB_{k}^{(f)}) denotes the existence of a protective belt for ministerial portfolio (k) within political framework (f), and (\Delta C_{k}^{(f)}) denotes the continuity differential of portfolio (k) relative to the other portfolios operating under the same political framework.


These two empirical implications distinguish two fundamentally different sources of variation. Proposition~\ref{prop:framework_ka} concerns historical variation across political frameworks. Proposition~\ref{prop:protective_belt} concerns organizational variation within political frameworks.



%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\subsection{Interpretation Heuristics}

The statistical analyses evaluate the empirical implications of Propositions~\ref{prop:framework_ka} and~\ref{prop:protective_belt}. Translating the resulting statistical patterns into statements about political development requires an interpretive framework. The heuristics presented here do not constitute additional propositions. Rather, they establish how the empirical results are interpreted with respect to the theoretical framework developed in the previous section.

For Proposition~\ref{prop:framework_ka}, the object of interpretation is the historical trajectory of continuity across successive political frameworks. Positive changes in continuity indicate increasingly favorable opportunities for knowledge accumulation, whereas negative changes indicate increasingly unfavorable opportunities. Stable continuity indicates that opportunities for knowledge accumulation remain broadly unchanged across political frameworks, whereas oscillating trajectories indicate that such opportunities vary across successive political environments.

For Proposition~\ref{prop:protective_belt}, the object of interpretation is organizational differentiation. Persistent continuity differentials associated with particular ministerial portfolios are interpreted as evidence consistent with the existence of protective belts. The persistence, emergence, or disappearance of such continuity differentials across political frameworks provides the empirical basis for evaluating whether protective arrangements remain stable or evolve over time.

These heuristics distinguish two complementary dimensions of interpretation. Historical comparisons evaluate variation in continuity across political frameworks, whereas organizational comparisons evaluate variation in continuity across ministerial portfolios within political frameworks. Together, these two dimensions establish the empirical basis upon which the broader historical interpretation developed in the Discussion section is constructed.

The substantive historical interpretation of these empirical patterns draws upon the Critical Juncture tradition of comparative-historical analysis. Concepts such as political legacies, path dependence, contingent historical development, and near-miss critical junctures are therefore employed in the Discussion to interpret the observed trajectories of continuity rather than as additional causal propositions to be tested statistically.


%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Statistical Procedures}

The empirical implications of Propositions~\ref{prop:framework_ka} and~\ref{prop:protective_belt} are evaluated using standard methods from survival analysis. Because the outcome of interest is the duration of ministerial appointments until replacement, survival analysis provides an appropriate framework for characterizing continuity while accommodating right-censored observations.

Kaplan--Meier estimators are first employed to provide nonparametric descriptions of ministerial survival across political frameworks and ministerial portfolios. These survival functions offer descriptive representations of the continuity conditions associated with each comparison.

Differences between survival functions are subsequently evaluated using log-rank tests. These tests assess whether observed differences in continuity are statistically distinguishable across political frameworks and across ministerial portfolios.

Finally, Cox proportional hazards models are employed to estimate the relative hazard of ministerial replacement associated with political frameworks and ministerial portfolios. Hazard ratios are interpreted as relative risks of ministerial replacement, where lower hazards indicate more favorable continuity conditions. The Cox models complement the nonparametric analyses by quantifying the magnitude of observed differences in continuity.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Model Specification}

The statistical analyses mirror the two propositions developed in the theoretical framework. Proposition~\ref{prop:framework_ka} is evaluated by comparing continuity across successive political frameworks. Proposition~\ref{prop:protective_belt} is evaluated by comparing continuity across ministerial portfolios within political frameworks.

Kaplan--Meier estimators, log-rank tests, and Cox proportional hazards models are applied to both comparisons. The first evaluates historical variation in continuity, whereas the second evaluates organizational variation in continuity.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Data}

The empirical analysis employs an original dataset of ministerial appointments in Peru spanning the republican period from Independence through 2022. The unit of observation is an individual ministerial spell, beginning with formal appointment and ending with ministerial replacement or right censoring at the close of the observation period.

The empirical analysis proceeds in two stages. The primary comparison focuses on the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF) and the Presidency of the Council of Ministers (PCM), the two portfolios used to evaluate Proposition~\ref{prop:protective_belt}. A second stage extends the analysis to three additional portfolios---Interior, Health, and Education---to determine whether the continuity patterns observed in the primary comparison are specific to those ministries or characterize broader organizational patterns across the executive branch.

For each ministerial spell, the dataset records the dates of appointment and replacement, the corresponding ministerial portfolio, and the political framework under which the appointment occurred. Political frameworks are assigned according to the operationalization presented in Table~\ref{tab:frameworks}. The selected portfolios retain sufficiently comparable governmental functions across successive political frameworks to permit meaningful historical comparison.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Limitations}

The research design evaluates observable implications rather than theoretical constructs directly. Political frameworks, knowledge accumulation, institutional learning, and protective belts are theoretical constructs whose empirical evaluation relies upon heuristic operationalizations. Continuity is inferred indirectly from ministerial duration rather than observed directly.

Accordingly, the statistical analyses do not estimate the causal effect of continuity on knowledge accumulation. Instead, they evaluate whether observed historical and organizational patterns of ministerial continuity are consistent with the empirical implications derived from Propositions~\ref{prop:framework_ka} and~\ref{prop:protective_belt}. The broader implications of these patterns for political development are developed in the Discussion.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Continuity Conditions Within Institutional Epochs}

The first proposition of the theoretical framework argued that continuity creates opportunities for knowledge accumulation within institutional environments. As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:research_design}, this proposition is not evaluated as a direct empirical test. Instead, it provides the interpretive foundation through which ministerial duration acquires substantive meaning. The empirical objective of this section is therefore descriptive: to characterize the continuity conditions observed within each institutional epoch.

Table~\ref{tab:epoch_descriptives} presents summary statistics for ministerial duration across the five institutional epochs identified in the historical-institutionalist framework. The results indicate substantial variation in continuity conditions across institutional epochs. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:epoch_descriptives}, median ministerial duration ranges from 118 days during State Formation to more than 300 days during both the Power-Sharing State and the Transition and Crisis periods. The interquartile ranges reveal similarly pronounced differences in the distribution of ministerial tenure across historical contexts. These patterns suggest that ministers operated under markedly different continuity conditions throughout Peru's republican history.

The State Formation and Oligarchic State epochs exhibit the weakest continuity conditions among the consolidated institutional periods. Median ministerial durations remained below five months in both epochs (118 and 131 days respectively), and half of all appointments lasted less than approximately nine months. These patterns are consistent with frequent ministerial replacement and limited opportunities for sustained administrative experience.

By contrast, The Power-Sharing State displays the strongest continuity conditions observed among the institutional epochs examined here. The median duration reached 311 days, with the upper quartile extending beyond 500 days. The Transition and Crisis period exhibits a remarkably similar pattern despite its association with economic collapse, political violence, and institutional instability. In both epochs, half of all ministerial appointments survived for approximately ten months or longer, suggesting substantially more favorable continuity conditions than those observed during the nineteenth century.

The Democracy without Parties epoch occupies an intermediate position. Its median duration of 259 days exceeds that of both State Formation and the Oligarchic State but remains below the levels observed during the Power-Sharing State and Transition and Crisis periods. Rather than representing either exceptionally strong continuity or chronic instability, the period appears to combine elements of both.

Finally, the Current Critical Juncture exhibits a sharp decline in continuity conditions. The median ministerial duration falls to 125 days, a level comparable to those observed during the State Formation and Oligarchic State epochs. Although the limited number of observations and the ongoing nature of the period caution against strong conclusions, the descriptive evidence suggests a substantial deterioration in continuity relative to the preceding epoch.

The figure reveals substantially greater variation across epochs than across portfolios. State Formation and the Oligarchic State exhibit relatively short ministerial tenures, whereas the Power-Sharing State and the Transition and Crisis period display markedly longer durations. Democracy without Parties occupies an intermediate position. The Current Critical Juncture shows a pronounced decline in duration, with continuity levels resembling those observed during the dawn of the Republican era in the nineteenth century. Across most epochs, PCM and MEF display broadly similar continuity patterns, suggesting that historical context exerts a stronger influence on ministerial survival than portfolio-specific characteristics.

This descriptive pattern provides the empirical basis for the formal comparisons presented in the following section.


\subsection{Institutional Learning Between Institutional Epochs}

Proposition~2 extends the descriptive analysis by asking whether continuity conditions vary systematically across institutional epochs in a manner consistent with institutional learning. If administrative experience accumulates and is preserved through successive institutional orders, later epochs should provide increasingly favorable conditions for ministerial continuity. Under this expectation, ministerial survival should broadly follow the chronological sequence of institutional development.

The descriptive evidence presented in the previous section revealed substantial variation in ministerial continuity across historical periods. However, visual differences in survival curves do not establish whether the observed patterns reflect systematic institutional variation or random fluctuations in ministerial tenure. This section therefore evaluates Proposition~2 using formal statistical comparisons of ministerial survival across institutional epochs.




\subsubsection{Comparing Survival Across Institutional Epochs}

The first step is to evaluate whether the survival functions observed across institutional epochs differ more than would be expected by chance. The Kaplan--Meier curves presented in Figure~\ref{fig:km_epochs} suggested substantial differences in ministerial continuity, but visual inspection alone cannot establish whether those differences are statistically meaningful.

To assess this, I use a log-rank test comparing ministerial survival across institutional epochs. The log-rank test evaluates the null hypothesis that all epochs share the same survival function. In substantive terms, this means testing whether ministers face similar replacement risks across historical periods. A statistically significant result indicates that continuity conditions differ across epochs, although it does not by itself identify the direction or magnitude of those differences. Direction and magnitude are examined in the Cox models presented in the next subsection.

Table~\ref{tab:logrank_epoch} reports the results of the log-rank test comparing ministerial survival across institutional epochs. The null hypothesis that ministers experience identical survival functions across institutional epochs is strongly rejected ($\chi^2 = 46.47$, $df = 5$, $p < .001$). This finding indicates that continuity conditions differed systematically across Peru's institutional history and confirms that the differences observed in the Kaplan--Meier curves are unlikely to reflect random variation alone.

The log-rank test, however, is a global test of equality and does not identify which institutional epochs exhibit higher or lower ministerial replacement risks. Nor does it quantify the magnitude of these differences. To address these questions, the next subsection estimates Cox proportional hazards models, which provide relative hazard ratios for each institutional epoch while preserving the time-to-event structure of the data.


\subsubsection{Cox Models of Institutional Learning}

While the log-rank test establishes that ministerial survival differs across institutional epochs, it does not quantify the direction or magnitude of those differences. To identify the direction and magnitude of these differences, I estimate Cox proportional hazards models in which institutional epoch serves as the principal explanatory variable. The Cox model estimates the relative hazard of ministerial replacement while making no assumptions about the underlying baseline hazard function. Hazard ratios below one indicate lower risks of ministerial replacement—and therefore stronger continuity conditions—relative to the reference epoch, whereas hazard ratios above one indicate weaker continuity conditions.

Table~\ref{tab:cox_epoch} quantifies the differences in ministerial replacement risks relative to the Democracy without Parties epoch, which serves as the reference category. Hazard ratios greater than one indicate a higher risk of ministerial replacement—and therefore weaker continuity conditions—whereas hazard ratios below one indicate lower replacement risks and stronger continuity conditions.

The results reinforce the descriptive patterns observed in the previous section. Relative to the Democracy without Parties epoch, ministers serving during State Formation experienced a 71\% higher hazard of replacement ($HR = 1.71$, $p < .001$), while those serving during the Oligarchic State faced a 49\% higher hazard ($HR = 1.49$, $p = .006$). Relative to the Democracy without Parties epoch, the Current Critical Juncture exhibits the highest estimated replacement risk, with ministers facing more than twice the hazard of replacement ($HR = 2.06$, $p = .005$).

By contrast, ministers serving during the Power-Sharing State ($HR = 0.82$) and the Transition and Crisis period ($HR = 0.85$) exhibit lower estimated replacement risks than those serving during the Democracy without Parties epoch. Although neither estimate reaches conventional levels of statistical significance, both hazard ratios are below one and are consistent with the ordering suggested by the Kaplan--Meier survival functions.

Taken together, the Cox estimates support the conclusion that institutional epochs are associated with systematically different continuity conditions. However, the estimated hazard ratios do not conform to the monotonic progression expected under a cumulative institutional learning process. Instead, they suggest that continuity conditions improved and deteriorated across successive institutional orders, with the strongest continuity observed during the Power-Sharing State and the weakest during the Current Critical Juncture.

\textbf{Assessment of Proposition 2}. The evidence provides partial support for the institutional learning proposition. Continuity conditions differ systematically across institutional epochs, indicating that historical institutional contexts shape ministerial survival. However, the observed pattern does not follow the monotonic progression expected under cumulative institutional learning. Rather than increasing steadily across successive institutional orders, continuity conditions fluctuate considerably, with the strongest continuity observed during the Power-Sharing State and the weakest during the Current Critical Juncture. These findings suggest that opportunities for institutional learning depend on the political and institutional characteristics of each historical order rather than accumulating monotonically across successive institutional epochs.
