\abstract{Why do political systems that change so much change so 
little in how they govern? This paper addresses that question 
through an original dataset of 1,062 ministerial spells covering 
five portfolios in Peru from 1821 to 2026. Rather than treating 
ministerial duration as the outcome to be explained, we treat it 
as a signal: an observable trace of the continuity conditions 
under which governing experience either accumulates into 
institutional knowledge or is repeatedly lost. We organize the 
analysis around two analytical heuristics. The first is the 
institutional epoch --- the path-dependent period between 
critical junctures --- which captures whether successive 
historical ruptures produced durable improvements in continuity 
conditions. The second is the protective belt --- a localized 
architecture of external accountability relationships that, where 
it exists, insulates certain ministries from the political logic 
governing the rest of the cabinet. Kaplan--Meier survival curves, 
log-rank tests, and Cox proportional hazards models show that 
epochs explain ministerial survival far better than individual 
traits or portfolio membership, and that continuity conditions 
did not improve monotonically across two centuries. The largest 
shift in the data occurred between the Oligarchic State and the 
Power-Sharing epoch; the most recent democratic period represents 
a partial regression toward the founding pattern of chronic 
interruption. The protective belt emerges only after 1993, when 
external accountability structures raised the cost of Economy 
minister replacement beyond what domestic political pressure 
alone could sustain. The central theoretical finding is that 
chronic ministerial instability in Peru is not evidence of absent 
institutions but of a durable informal one: the escape valve 
through which political pressure is absorbed without producing 
structural change. The instability itself is the institution.}

\section*{Introduction}
% ============================================================

Governing is hard. Education, health, and public safety are not technical
problems with known solutions --- they are complex social challenges that
resist quick fixes and demand sustained institutional effort. Good policy
takes time to design, time to implement, and more time still to reach the
people it is meant to serve. Good practices percolate gradually. Organizations learn slowly.  Citizens feel change only after years of consistent effort in the same direction. In the big picture, where governance improved, it did so through institutional correction after crisis --- converting rupture into consolidation, learning from failure rather than repeating it.

After more than two hundred years of republican life, the Peruvian political class has repeatedly changed governments, cabinets, constitutions, and political coalitions. Yet the underlying pattern remains remarkably stable. Each new administration appoints a cabinet whose members understand, implicitly or explicitly, the terms of their engagement: they are expected to manage complexity on behalf of the leader, but they may also be sacrificed whenever political pressure demands a visible response. Their departure rarely resolves the conflict that triggered it. The resignation of a premier or any other minister may relieve immediate pressure, but it seldom addresses the underlying causes of crisis. Cabinet reshuffles signal responsiveness without necessarily producing institutional correction.

This persistence should not be mistaken for an absence of learning. Political actors adapt continuously. Over time, they have become increasingly adept at navigating constitutional ambiguities, managing blame, and deploying ministerial replacement as a mechanism for absorbing public dissatisfaction. If the political class has learned anything, it has been how to reproduce the system's response to crisis rather than how to transform it. Governments change. Ministers change. The repertoire through which instability is managed remains largely intact. This cycle has marked much of Peru's republican history.



This vicious circle is generally called path dependence --- a sophisticated way to say that the inertia of accumulated choices makes deviation increasingly costly and increasingly unlikely. And yet paths do break. Oxford philosopher Roman \textcite{krznaric_how_2024} argues that radical change requires what he calls the disruption nexus: a crisis, disruptive movements that amplify it, and powerful ideas that are ready to fill the space the crisis opens. France's Fifth Republic had all three: the Algerian War as crisis, De~Gaulle's movement as disruptive force, and a constitutional design --- the strong executive --- as the idea ready to be deployed. It was not one event that broke France's path. It was a multigenerational accumulation of chronic instability that the Third and Fourth Republics never resolved. The Fifth Republic became the enduring legacy of a critical juncture in French history. More importantly, it appears to have altered the conditions under which political continuity was produced. The result was not merely institutional change, but a durable shift in the patterns of executive stability that had characterized French politics for more than a century.



France, before the Fifth Republic, was not very different from Peru during the previous hundred years. Both experienced remarkably high levels of executive instability, reflected in similar numbers of premier appointments per decade. Figure~\ref{fig:premiers} tells the story visually. Peru peaked at 23 appointments in the 1890s; France reached 20 in the 1930s. Yet the advent of the Fifth Republic in 1958 initiated sharply diverging trajectories. French premier appointments fell to a stable band of three to six per decade and have remained there ever since, while Peru is returning to levels observed in the nineteenth century. Peru has experienced no shortage of potential disruption nexuses --- or, as we will later call them, critical junctures: independence without state-building, the War of the Pacific, hyperinflation, terrorism, the fall of Fujimori, and a permanent constitutional crisis since 2016. Each represented a moment in which alternative institutional trajectories became plausible.

The question, however, is not whether institutional change occurred. Peru's republican history is full of reforms, constitutions, regime transitions, and political ruptures. The question is whether those ruptures produced institutional learning. By institutional learning, we mean the capacity of an institutional order to generate more favorable conditions for preserving, accumulating, and transmitting political experience over time. From this perspective, a critical juncture is successful not simply because it produces a new institutional arrangement, but because it leaves behind a legacy that alters how the system responds to future crises. France appears to have experienced such a transformation after 1958. Peru's trajectory raises the possibility that institutional change and institutional learning do not necessarily coincide.

I approach institutional learning through ministerial duration data using the heuristic of institutional epochs. Within epochs, duration provides evidence about the conditions under which knowledge accumulates; across epochs, changes in those patterns provide evidence about institutional learning. Political science has traditionally studied these phenomena separately. Ministerial duration has been analyzed through the tools of survival and event-history analysis, while institutional change has been examined through the comparative-historical literature on critical junctures and path dependence. This paper combines both traditions. It uses ministerial duration data to evaluate whether critical junctures produced enduring changes in the conditions under which political experience accumulates.

The  \textit{epoch} heuristic is the institutional period that encompasses one or more presidential administrations and is defined by the critical junctures that periodically restructure the state's political logic. Epochs capture what neither individual traits nor political context alone can explain: the continuity conditions under which experience is either accumulated or repeatedly lost. If institutional learning occurs, it should be observable through changes in those conditions across successive epochs.

Within epochs, this work conceptualizes a  second analytical heuristic, the \textit{protective belt}: an \emph{ad hoc} architecture of rules, actors, and external accountability relationships that, where it exists, helps preserve continuity despite the political logic governing the rest of the cabinet. By protecting continuity, the belt creates opportunities for knowledge accumulation within epochs and may allow certain sectors to function as islands of institutional effectiveness within a broader environment of instability. The protective belt does not explain institutional learning at the level of the state as a whole. Rather, it explains why some ministries may accumulate knowledge and preserve continuity even when the broader institutional environment remains unfavorable.

The question guiding this study is straightforward: \emph{Have Peru's critical junctures ever produced institutional learning?}

This question cannot be answered directly. Institutional learning is not observed in the data. Instead, the study approaches the question indirectly through patterns of ministerial duration. If institutional learning improves the conditions under which political experience accumulates and is preserved, then such improvements should be reflected in changes in ministerial continuity across institutional epochs. Ministerial duration therefore provides an approximate indicator of the continuity conditions from which institutional learning may emerge.

The theoretical framework developed in this paper distinguishes between knowledge accumulation within institutional epochs and institutional learning across them. This distinction allows the central question to be translated into three empirical questions that can be addressed with duration data. \emph{First}, what continuity conditions for knowledge accumulation characterized each institutional epoch? \emph{Second}, did those continuity conditions change across successive epochs in ways consistent with institutional learning, or did critical junctures repeatedly reproduce the same underlying pattern of ministerial instability? \emph{Third}, do some sectors of the state systematically escape the continuity conditions of their epoch, exhibiting survival patterns consistent with the presence of a protective belt, and if so, what does their existence reveal about the broader dynamics of continuity, knowledge accumulation, and institutional effectiveness?


To answer these questions, we construct an original dataset of individual ministerial spells covering the period from 1821 to 2026. The dataset includes the portfolios most relevant to the argument developed here: the premier, who coordinates the cabinet and sustains the political viability of the executive; the economy minister, whose decisions shape the state's fiscal and investment capacity; and the ministers of Interior, Health, and Education, whose portfolios address some of the most consequential and persistent public challenges facing the country, according to official yearly surveys \parencite{inei_encuesta_2024}. The spells are organized into six institutional epochs defined by the critical junctures that periodically restructured Peru's political logic \parencite{lopez_jimenez_peru_2025}. Combining descriptive analysis with survival models, the study evaluates whether patterns of ministerial continuity reveal different conditions for knowledge accumulation within epochs, whether those conditions changed across successive epochs, and whether some sectors exhibit continuity patterns consistent with the presence of a protective belt.

\subsection{Trait-Based Explanations of Ministerial Survival}

Trait-based approaches explain ministerial survival through characteristics of the officeholder. Across different institutional settings, scholars have shown that ministers' professional backgrounds, political careers, partisan affiliations, and personal resources influence their probability of remaining in office. Early studies emphasized broad distinctions between managerial and parliamentary profiles, as well as the effects of career trajectories and prior political experience \parencite{blondel_ministerial_1988}. Subsequent work expanded the range of relevant characteristics to include age, education, gender, portfolio rank, and seniority within government \parencite{fischer_duration_2012,dewan_corrective_2005}.

More recent research has refined these categories by focusing on the organizational and political resources that ministers bring to office. Feliú Ribeiro and López Burian show that technical expertise and membership in presidential networks increase ministerial survival, while prior electoral experience may paradoxically operate as a risk factor \parencite{feliu_ribeiro_foreign_2023}. Across four Latin American countries between 1945 and 2020, career politicians proved more vulnerable to dismissal than technocrats. The finding suggests that ministerial value cannot be reduced to political experience alone; survival depends on the type of resources that political leaders seek to preserve.

The literature has also moved beyond simple dichotomies between partisan and non-partisan ministers. Camerlo and Castaldo argue that partisan affiliation conceals substantial variation in the strength of a minister's relationship with the party organization. Their framework distinguishes among leaders, high-ranks, low-ranks, newcomers, shadows, and defectors according to levels of engagement, consolidation, and hierarchical position within the party \parencite{camerlo_government_2024}. Applied to the Argentine cabinet under Alberto Fernández, this approach reveals that apparently strong partisan cabinets may actually consist of ministers with limited organizational leverage over their parties \parencite{camerlo_poderes_2024}. For survival analysis, the implication is straightforward: ministers classified under the same partisan label may possess very different capacities to resist political pressure.

Taken together, these studies demonstrate that ministerial duration is shaped by who ministers are. Technical expertise, organizational embeddedness, political experience, and personal resources all influence survival prospects. Yet the significance of these traits depends on the environments in which ministers operate. The same characteristic may be rewarded in one institutional setting and penalized in another. This observation leads to the second major family of explanations.

\subsection{Context-Based Explanations of Ministerial Survival}

Context-based approaches explain ministerial survival through characteristics of the political environment rather than characteristics of individual ministers. In this perspective, ministers survive or fail because of the institutional and political constraints surrounding them.

The comparative literature consistently identifies coalition dynamics as a central determinant of ministerial duration. Coalition type, minority government status, electoral volatility, and party-system fragmentation all shape the likelihood of ministerial replacement independently of individual characteristics \parencite{huber_replacing_2008}. Similarly, political crises, scandals, protests, electoral calendars, and term limits alter the incentives presidents face when deciding whether to retain or dismiss ministers \parencite{camerlo_minister_2015,alexiadou_commitment_2019}. Even macroeconomic performance influences survival probabilities by modifying the broader political environment within which governments operate \parencite{feliu_ribeiro_foreign_2023}.

The case literature illustrates these mechanisms particularly clearly. For Colombia, Mejía Guinand et al.\ find that individual characteristics matter, but the institutional configuration of the political system ultimately determines the range within which those characteristics can operate \parencite{mejia_es_2021}. Constitutional reforms, changes in party-system structure, and coalition arrangements alter the political logic governing ministerial careers. A similar conclusion emerges from Chile. Avendaño and Dávila show that ministerial turnover during the Concertación governments was driven primarily by coalition management considerations rather than by individual performance. Finance ministers consistently exhibited the longest tenures regardless of personal characteristics \parencite{avendano_rotacion_2012}.

Recent work has also refined the contextual analysis of ministerial portfolios. Portfolio importance has traditionally been measured through expert rankings that assign ministries a single level of salience. Camerlo and Martínez-Gallardo argue that such measures obscure important differences among ministries, particularly in contexts characterized by weak party institutionalization \parencite{camerlo_por_2022}. Their framework decomposes ministerial value into policy-management capacity, discretional resource allocation, political agenda influence, and inter-portfolio coordination authority. Different political environments activate different dimensions of portfolio value, producing distinct survival incentives for presidents and ministers alike.

Taken together, these studies show that ministerial survival depends not only on who ministers are but also on where they operate. Institutional structures, coalition arrangements, political crises, economic conditions, and portfolio characteristics shape the opportunities and constraints governing ministerial careers.

\subsection{From Ministerial Survival to Institutional Learning}

The two families of explanations are not competing research programs. Virtually every study combines individual and contextual covariates, with political environments conditioning the effects of ministerial traits. The resulting literature has generated a rich understanding of why some ministers survive longer than others.

What the literature has not asked is a prior question: what survival time itself reveals about the institutional environment that produces it. Existing studies treat duration as the outcome to be explained. This paper treats duration as a signal. Before asking why one minister survives longer than another, we ask whether patterns of ministerial continuity reveal differences in the conditions under which governing experience accumulates and is preserved over time. In this perspective, survival is not the phenomenon of interest but an observable trace of a deeper process.

The next section develops a theoretical framework linking continuity, knowledge accumulation, and institutional learning, and introduces critical junctures and institutional epochs as the analytical devices through which those processes can be studied historically.


\section{Theoretical Framework}

\subsection{From Ministerial Survival to Institutional Learning}

The literature reviewed above treats ministerial survival as the outcome to be explained. This paper treats it as a signal. What survival time carries, we argue, is information about a prior and more consequential process: the accumulation of institutional knowledge within the state. A foundational claim of organizational learning theory is that institutions outperform alternatives when they successfully encode inferences from experience into routines that guide future behavior \parencite[319--320]{levitt_organizational_1988}. For this to occur, experience must first persist long enough to be accumulated, shared, and embedded within organizations. Ministerial stability is therefore not valuable in itself. Rather, it constitutes a necessary condition for the accumulation of knowledge from which broader processes of institutional learning may subsequently emerge \parencite{nonaka_knowledge-creating_1998}.

For the purposes of this paper, it is useful to distinguish between \textbf{knowledge accumulation} and \textbf{institutional learning}. Knowledge accumulation refers to the process through which experience is retained, articulated, shared, and embedded within organizations. Institutional learning refers to the capacity of an institutional order to generate progressively more favorable conditions for the accumulation, retention, and transmission of experience over time. The two concepts are related but distinct. Knowledge accumulation occurs within organizations and within institutional epochs. Institutional learning becomes visible when successive institutional orders improve the conditions under which such accumulation can occur.

This distinction draws on a common insight running through the literature reviewed below. For Nonaka, learning requires the successful conversion of experience into organizational knowledge \parencite{nonaka_knowledge-creating_1998}. For Simon, organizations learn when they develop more effective ways of structuring and responding to complex problems \parencite{foss_simon_2005}. For Allison, learning becomes visible when experience is embedded in routines that guide future action \parencite{allison_conceptual_1969}. Across these perspectives, learning implies more than isolated episodes of knowledge creation. It implies the development of conditions that allow experience to be retained and used rather than repeatedly lost.

This distinction has important implications for interpretation. Survival is not treated as a direct measure of either knowledge accumulation or institutional learning. A long ministerial tenure does not demonstrate that knowledge accumulated, just as a short tenure does not prove that accumulation failed. Rather, survival is interpreted as an observable indicator of continuity. Continuity creates opportunities for knowledge accumulation, and knowledge accumulation creates the conditions from which institutional learning may eventually emerge. What survival time reveals, therefore, is not learning itself but the extent to which institutional continuity permits experience to accumulate.

Reading survival as a signal of continuity has one methodological implication that separates this paper from the existing literature. Duration cannot be modeled as a homogeneous process pooled across history. If continuity is the mechanism through which knowledge accumulates, then the relevant question is whether the conditions supporting that accumulation changed over time. This requires analytical cut points corresponding to moments when the institutional logic governing political life was transformed. Such moments are identified through the critical-juncture framework developed below. Critical junctures delimit institutional epochs, and epochs constitute the historical environments within which continuity either facilitates knowledge accumulation or repeatedly interrupts it.

Consequently, the objective of this study is not to determine whether individual ministers learned, nor whether particular appointments were successful. Instead, it evaluates whether different institutional epochs generated different continuity conditions and, therefore, different opportunities for knowledge accumulation. Ministerial survival serves as the observable trace of those conditions. The question is not why ministers survive, but what patterns of survival reveal about the state's capacity to accumulate and preserve experience across successive historical periods.

The remainder of the theoretical framework develops this argument in three steps. First, it explains the mechanisms through which continuity enables knowledge accumulation within institutional epochs. Second, it examines how changes in those continuity conditions across epochs can be interpreted as evidence of institutional learning. Third, it introduces the concept of the protective belt to explain why some ministries may experience continuity conditions that differ from those prevailing in the broader institutional environment.

\subsection{Knowledge Accumulation Within Epochs}

If ministerial duration is interpreted as a signal of continuity, the first theoretical task is to explain how continuity contributes to knowledge accumulation. The question is therefore straightforward: what mechanisms allow governing experience to accumulate, and what mechanisms interrupt that process?

The argument developed here begins with a simple proposition: knowledge accumulation depends on continuity over time. Drawing on Nonaka, knowledge creation is not a single event but a cumulative process through which tacit knowledge becomes explicit, is shared within the organization, and is eventually internalized into routines and practices \parencite{nonaka_knowledge-creating_1998}. New knowledge emerges through repeated interaction among organizational members, the articulation of experience into formal procedures, and the gradual incorporation of those procedures into everyday behavior. The process cannot be compressed into a single decision, nor can it survive repeated interruptions without loss.

\begin{spacing}{1.5}
\noindent
\texttt{Continuity}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{\bf Knowledge Accumulation}
\end{spacing}

The first connection links Nonaka's theory of knowledge creation to Cohen, March, and Olsen's theory of organized anarchy. Organized anarchies are characterized by problematic preferences, unclear technology, and fluid participation \parencite{cohen_garbage_1972}. High ministerial turnover operationalizes fluid participation at the cabinet level: decision-makers are replaced before they can develop a shared understanding of the problems they were appointed to address. From a causal perspective, fluid participation weakens continuity by preventing the emergence of the shared cognitive environment required for knowledge creation.

\begin{spacing}{1.5}
\noindent
\texttt{Fluid Participation}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{Continuity}
\end{spacing}

The second connection links Nonaka's model to Simon's theory of bounded rationality. Complex policy problems initially present themselves as ill-structured and become manageable only through gradual processes of problem decomposition and constraint formation \parencite[4--5]{foss_simon_2005}. When ministers confront complex social challenges, their understanding is initially tacit. For organizations to benefit from that experience, tacit knowledge must be articulated, codified, and transformed into explicit knowledge that can be communicated and shared. This transformation requires time. Premature dismissal interrupts the conversion process and leaves accumulated experience personal rather than institutional.

\begin{spacing}{1.5}
\noindent
\texttt{Knowledge Articulation}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{\bf Knowledge Accumulation}
\end{spacing}

The third connection links Nonaka's model to Allison's organizational process perspective. Governments respond to recurring problems through standard operating procedures that have been developed through prior experience \parencite{allison_conceptual_1969}. Once knowledge has been articulated and formalized, it must be repeatedly applied before it becomes embedded in organizational routines. Chronic turnover disrupts this final stage by preventing newly created knowledge from stabilizing within organizational practice.

\begin{spacing}{1.5}
\noindent
\texttt{\bf Knowledge Accumulation}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{Routine Formation}
\end{spacing}

From this perspective, organizational routines constitute the institutional repository through which accumulated knowledge survives individual officeholders.

\begin{spacing}{1.5}
\noindent
\texttt{Routine Formation}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{\bf Organizational Memory}
\end{spacing}

Taken together, these perspectives describe a common mechanism of failure. Cohen, March, and Olsen explain why the social conditions required for knowledge creation may never emerge. Simon explains why the articulation of knowledge requires sustained attention to complex problems. Allison explains why newly created knowledge must be embedded in routines before organizations can benefit from it. Nonaka provides the connective logic linking these processes. The result is a single causal argument: continuity enables the articulation, accumulation, and retention of knowledge, while chronic turnover repeatedly interrupts each stage of the process.

\begin{spacing}{1.5}
\noindent
\textbf{Within Epochs:}

\texttt{Continuity}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{\bf Knowledge Accumulation}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{Organizational Memory}
\end{spacing}

\subsection{Institutional Learning Between Epochs}

The previous section explained how knowledge accumulates within institutional epochs. Institutional learning, however, refers to something different. It concerns whether the conditions governing knowledge accumulation themselves change over time. If continuity facilitates the retention of experience within epochs, institutional learning becomes visible when successive institutional orders generate more favorable conditions for that continuity.



Explaining such changes requires a theory of historical discontinuity. The comparative-historical literature provides such a framework through the concept of the critical juncture.

Critical junctures are defined as relatively short periods of time during which there is a substantially heightened probability that agents' choices will affect the outcome of interest \parencite[348]{capoccia_study_2007}. These brief moments of structural fluidity are followed by long periods of path-dependent institutional reproduction, in which the choices made during the juncture become increasingly difficult to reverse. The standard application of this framework identifies a formal institution created or transformed at the juncture and traces its reproduction across the subsequent path. For our purposes, the implication is theoretical: if knowledge accumulates or is repeatedly interrupted within institutional periods, then the appropriate unit of analysis is the epoch --- the path-dependent phase between junctures --- rather than the presidential term or the individual ministerial spell.

\begin{spacing}{1.5}
\noindent
\texttt{\bf Critical Juncture}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{Institutional Epoch}
\end{spacing}

Capoccia introduces a further concept that proves essential for the framework: the near-miss critical juncture. Because critical junctures are defined by contingency --- by what happened in the context of what could have happened --- it is entirely possible for a crisis to produce a moment of genuine institutional openness that is ultimately resolved through re-equilibration rather than change \parencite[350--351]{capoccia_study_2007}. The previous institutional arrangement is reinstated, often in modified form, and the path continues. Critical junctures may therefore fail to alter the conditions governing knowledge accumulation. Institutional change can occur without a corresponding transformation in learning conditions.

This observation creates a theoretical puzzle. If formal institutions are repeatedly transformed, disrupted, or replaced, in what sense can one speak of path dependence at all? Levitsky and Murillo argue that in much of the developing world actors develop expectations of instability rather than expectations of institutional reproduction \parencite[123]{levitsky_variation_2009}. Yet from the perspective developed here, the relevant question is not whether formal institutions remain unchanged, but whether the conditions governing knowledge accumulation persist across time. Informal mechanisms may survive repeated formal transformations and thereby reproduce the same learning conditions across successive epochs.

Institutional learning becomes visible when critical
junctures alter the continuity conditions governing
knowledge accumulation in durable ways. A sequence of critical junctures followed by increasingly favorable conditions for continuity would suggest institutional learning. A sequence of critical junctures followed by repeated interruptions or unchanged continuity conditions would suggest its absence. The key theoretical question is therefore whether successive institutional epochs generated different opportunities for knowledge accumulation.

\begin{spacing}{1.5}
\noindent
\textbf{Between Epochs:}

\texttt{\bf Critical Juncture}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{Institutional Epoch}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{\bf Institutional Learning}
\end{spacing}

\subsection{The Exception That Tests the Rule: The Protective Belt}

The previous sections described the general mechanisms governing knowledge accumulation within epochs and institutional learning across them. Yet continuity conditions are not necessarily distributed uniformly throughout the state. Some ministries may experience survival patterns that differ systematically from those prevailing in the broader institutional environment. Explaining such variation requires an additional mechanism.

The concept proposed here is the \textbf{Protective Belt}. Borrowing the structural logic of Lakatos's philosophy of science, the belt functions as a buffer that absorbs pressures that would otherwise interrupt continuity \parencite{lakatos_methodology_1978}. It consists of informal rules, external actors, and accountability relationships that raise the political cost of ministerial replacement and thereby reduce the likelihood that continuity will be interrupted.

\begin{spacing}{1.5}
\noindent
\texttt{Protective Belt}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{Continuity}
$\rightarrow$
\texttt{\bf Knowledge Accumulation}
\end{spacing}

The concept draws on comparative evidence showing that some portfolios systematically exhibit greater ministerial survival than others. Feliú Ribeiro and López Burian demonstrate that foreign policy portfolios survive significantly longer than domestic portfolios across Latin America \parencite{feliu_ribeiro_foreign_2023}. The mechanism they identify is external accountability. International audiences create credibility costs that presidents must absorb when replacing ministers, thereby increasing continuity independently of domestic political pressures.

The protective belt is therefore not a veto player, nor a form of social capital. It is a cost structure that alters the incentives surrounding ministerial replacement. Its presence or absence determines whether the continuity conditions experienced by a ministry differ from those prevailing in the broader institutional environment.

The Protective Belt does not explain institutional learning at the level of the state as a whole. Rather, it explains why some ministries may accumulate knowledge and preserve continuity even when broader learning conditions remain unfavorable. It is a localized continuity-preserving mechanism that may create islands of effectiveness without altering the broader learning conditions of the institutional order.

The epochs summarized above are not merely chronological containers. Each represents a distinct institutional environment governing the conditions under which political experience could be accumulated, transmitted, or interrupted. These cut points correspond to the completion of the critical juncture rather than to its onset, ensuring continuous temporal coverage of ministerial spells while avoiding the assignment of transitional years to a stable institutional order.


If institutional learning occurred across Peru's republican history, it should 
be observable through changes in continuity conditions across these successive 
epochs. A sequence of epochs generating progressively longer ministerial 
survival would suggest improving learning conditions; a sequence reproducing 
the same patterns of interruption would suggest their absence. Because the 
present study evaluates institutional learning through changes in continuity 
conditions across completed epochs, the post-2022 period is analyzed separately 
as an ongoing process rather than as a consolidated institutional epoch.

\subsection{Why Not Constitutional Periods or Regime Types?}

Two alternative periodization strategies present themselves naturally 
and deserve explicit consideration. The first would organize ministerial 
spells by constitutional period. Peru has enacted thirteen constitutions 
since independence, each representing a formal institutional rupture. The 
second would organize spells by regime type --- distinguishing democratic 
from authoritarian or military periods --- following the approach standard 
in comparative survival studies \parencite{feliu_ribeiro_foreign_2023}.

Both alternatives are rejected on theoretical grounds. Constitutional 
dates in Peru are precisely the kind of formal institutional change that 
does not necessarily produce behavioral change. As Levitsky and Murillo 
argue, formal rules in weak institutional environments are often 
repeatedly overturned or rendered ineffective, with actors developing 
expectations of instability rather than compliance 
\parencite[123]{levitsky_variation_2009}. Peru's constitutional history 
exemplifies this pattern: thirteen constitutions in two centuries means 
that most constitutional moments did not produce durable path-dependent 
change in how political experience accumulates. Using constitutional dates 
as cut points would risk mistaking formal institutional change for 
substantive transformation in continuity conditions --- exactly the 
distinction this paper is built to capture.

Regime-type periodization presents a different problem. Classifying historical periods as democratic or authoritarian collapses variation in continuity conditions into a categorical distinction that is not central to the argument developed here. The theoretical framework focuses on the conditions under which political experience accumulates and is preserved. Those conditions may vary substantially within both democratic and authoritarian settings, depending on the broader configuration of political power, institutional incentives, and mechanisms of ministerial replacement. A regime classification therefore risks obscuring the very dimension of variation this study seeks to identify. By contrast, the epoch framework preserves continuity as the primary analytical dimension, allowing changes in knowledge-accumulation conditions to emerge from the historical trajectory of institutional development rather than from pre-defined regime categories. For this reason, the analysis follows the structural-historical periodization proposed by \textcite{lopez_jimenez_peru_2025}, which identifies epochs according to the critical junctures that reconfigured the state's political logic rather than according to formal constitutional or regime boundaries.


\subsection{Ministerial Spells as Units of Observation}

The fundamental unit of observation in this study is the ministerial spell: 
the uninterrupted period during which an individual occupies a specific 
cabinet portfolio. A spell begins on the date of appointment and ends on 
the date of departure --- whether by dismissal, resignation, death in 
office, or the termination of the presidential administration. This 
definition treats each continuous period of service as a discrete 
observation, independent of what came before or after. When the same 
individual serves multiple non-consecutive terms in the same or different 
portfolios, each appointment is coded as a separate spell. The reason is 
theoretical: each spell represents a distinct opportunity for continuity 
and knowledge accumulation, which may or may not be realized.

Duration is defined as the number of days elapsed between the start and 
end dates of each spell. This is not a measure of performance, influence, 
or effectiveness. It is a measure of time --- the raw material from which 
continuity is built or denied. A spell of three hundred days does not 
tell us that the minister learned anything, managed well, or left the 
portfolio better than they found it. It tells us that three hundred days 
were available for that process to begin. Conversely, a spell of thirty 
days tells us that the process was interrupted before it could advance 
beyond its earliest stages, regardless of the minister's individual 
qualities or intentions.

This distinction between duration and performance is essential for 
interpreting what follows. The analysis does not evaluate ministers. It 
evaluates the institutional environment that determined how long ministers 
were permitted to remain in office. Two ministers may be equally competent, 
equally committed, and equally well-connected, yet one serves for two years 
while the other is replaced after two months. The difference is not 
explained by their individual traits. It is explained by the political 
logic --- the escape valve or the protective belt --- governing the 
institutional environment in which they served.

Duration is therefore treated here as an institutional indicator rather 
than as an individual outcome. When aggregated across all spells within 
an epoch, the distribution of durations provides evidence about the 
continuity conditions that characterized that institutional period. When 
compared across epochs, changes in that distribution provide evidence 
about whether successive critical junctures altered those conditions in 
durable ways. And when compared across portfolios within the same epoch, 
differences in that distribution provide evidence about whether some 
ministries operated under systematically different continuity conditions 
than others --- the signature of the protective belt.

The database covers five portfolios spanning the period from 1821 to 2026, 
yielding 1,069 individual ministerial spells. The selected portfolios are 
not intended to represent the entirety of the Peruvian state. Rather, they 
capture positions central to the argument developed in this study. The 
premier coordinates cabinet action and sustains executive viability; the 
economy minister shapes fiscal and investment capacity; and the ministers 
of Interior, Health, and Education are responsible for policy areas 
consistently identified among citizens' principal concerns in official 
national surveys \parencite{inei_encuesta_2024}. Together, these portfolios 
capture both the state's capacity to govern and its capacity to address the 
public problems most visible to citizens. Table~\ref{tab:spells_portfolio}

\subsection{Ministerial Duration as an Indicator of Continuity}

The central empirical challenge of this study is that neither knowledge 
accumulation nor institutional learning can be observed directly. The 
available evidence consists of appointment and departure dates. Ministerial 
duration is therefore treated as an observable indicator of continuity 
rather than as a direct measure of learning.

This distinction follows directly from the theoretical framework. Knowledge 
accumulation requires that experience persist long enough to be articulated, 
transmitted, and embedded within organizational routines. Continuity does 
not guarantee that such processes occur, but repeated interruption makes 
them increasingly difficult. Ministerial duration consequently provides 
information about the temporal opportunities available for knowledge 
accumulation --- not proof that accumulation happened, but evidence about 
whether the conditions that make it possible were present.

The interpretation adopted here is therefore indirect and operates at two 
levels. At the level of individual spells, longer durations signal greater 
opportunity for experience to be retained and converted into organizational 
knowledge. At the aggregate level, the distribution of durations within an 
epoch characterizes the continuity conditions of that institutional period 
as a whole. Institutional learning is inferred not from the tenure of 
individual ministers but from systematic changes in those aggregate 
continuity conditions across successive epochs. A pattern of improving 
durations across epochs would be consistent with improving learning 
conditions; a pattern of stagnation or deterioration would suggest their 
absence.

\subsection{Analytical Expectations}

The argument developed in this paper generates observable expectations at 
two levels of analysis.

The first concerns institutional learning across epochs. If Peru's critical 
junctures produced durable improvements in the conditions governing 
knowledge accumulation, those improvements should be visible in the 
duration data. Specifically, ministerial spells should tend to be longer 
and the risk of replacement lower in epochs following junctures that 
altered the underlying political logic. Conversely, if critical junctures 
repeatedly failed to transform that logic --- if they were near-misses 
rather than genuine ruptures --- continuity conditions should remain 
largely unchanged across epochs despite major formal institutional 
transformations. In that case, the escape valve would continue to operate 
across historical periods, and no systematic improvement in duration 
patterns would be detectable.

The second concerns variation within epochs. The protective-belt heuristic 
suggests that some portfolios may partially escape the political dynamics 
affecting the rest of the cabinet. Where external accountability raises the 
cost of ministerial replacement, duration should be systematically longer 
in those portfolios than the general continuity conditions of the epoch 
would predict. The presence of such a pattern would not demonstrate 
institutional learning at the level of the state as a whole. Rather, it 
would indicate localized environments in which continuity and knowledge 
accumulation are more effectively protected --- islands of relative 
stability within a broader pattern of institutional interruption.

These expectations do not constitute deterministic hypotheses. Political 
systems are complex, and duration patterns reflect many factors beyond the 
mechanisms identified here. The expectations instead provide a framework 
for interpreting the evidence: they specify what improving learning 
conditions would look like in the data and what their absence would look 
like, allowing the analysis to evaluate whether the historical trajectory 
of ministerial continuity in Peru is consistent with the theoretical 
argument developed above.

\subsection{Analytical Strategy}

The empirical analysis proceeds in three stages, moving from description 
to estimation.

The first stage uses descriptive statistics to characterize ministerial 
continuity across portfolios and institutional epochs. Measures of central 
tendency --- particularly median duration --- and dispersion provide an 
initial overview of the continuity conditions associated with each 
historical period. Medians are preferred over means because duration 
distributions are typically right-skewed: a small number of very long 
spells can distort the mean substantially, while the median more 
accurately represents the typical minister's experience. At this stage, 
the analysis identifies whether broad patterns in the data are consistent 
with the theoretical expectations described above.

The second stage employs survival analysis to estimate the probability 
that a minister remains in office over time. Survival analysis is 
particularly well suited to duration data because it accounts for 
\textit{censored} observations --- spells that had not yet ended at the 
time the data were collected. Ignoring censoring would systematically 
underestimate how long ministers actually serve. The non-parametric 
Kaplan--Meier estimator produces survival curves showing, for any given 
number of days in office, the estimated proportion of ministers still 
serving. These curves can be computed separately for each epoch and each 
portfolio, and compared visually and statistically. Where the curves for 
different epochs or portfolios diverge, it suggests that the continuity 
conditions governing those groups differ systematically. Log-rank tests 
provide a formal statistical assessment of whether observed differences 
between curves are likely to reflect genuine variation or random 
fluctuation.

The third stage employs semi-parametric Cox proportional-hazards models 
to evaluate whether epoch and portfolio differences in ministerial survival 
persist after accounting for other observable characteristics of spells. 
The Cox model estimates the \textit{hazard} of ministerial exit --- that 
is, the instantaneous risk of departure at any given moment --- as a 
function of covariates. Unlike the Kaplan--Meier estimator, it allows 
multiple factors to be considered simultaneously, making it possible to 
assess whether the epoch and portfolio effects identified descriptively 
remain detectable once other influences are held constant. The objective 
is not to explain individual ministerial exits but to determine whether 
continuity conditions vary systematically across historical epochs and 
whether some portfolios exhibit survival patterns consistent with the 
protective-belt argument, independently of other observable features of 
the data.

Taken together, these three stages provide an indirect but systematic 
assessment of the theoretical argument: description establishes the 
patterns, survival curves compare them across groups, and Cox models 
evaluate their robustness. The evidence produced at each stage is 
interpreted not as proof of organizational learning but as evidence about 
whether the continuity conditions from which learning may emerge differed 
across Peru's institutional epochs and across its ministerial portfolios.

basic empirical landscape. The question is not yet whether observed 
differences are statistically significant or theoretically meaningful

minister rather than the outliers at either extreme. At the 25th

ministerial spells ended within three months of appointment. This

theoretical framework was not an occasional response to exceptional

\subsection{Have Peru's Critical Junctures Generated Different 
Continuity Conditions?}

The first research question asks whether Peru's institutional epochs 
generated different continuity conditions for knowledge accumulation, 
or whether successive critical junctures reproduced the same 
underlying pattern of ministerial instability. The descriptive 
patterns suggest variation across epochs, but raw medians do not 
account for the distribution of spell durations within each period 
or for the possibility that observed differences reflect random 
fluctuation rather than genuine institutional change. Survival 
analysis addresses both limitations.

The results are consistent across both specifications. Relative to 
State Formation, the Oligarchic State shows no significant reduction 
in exit hazard (HR = 1.03, $p$ = 0.72 in Model~3). The 
Power-Sharing State produces a large and highly significant 
reduction (HR = 0.529, $p$ $<$ 0.001) --- ministers in this epoch 
faced roughly half the exit risk of their counterparts in the 
founding period. The Transition epoch maintained this improvement 
almost exactly (HR = 0.524, $p$ $<$ 0.001). The Democracy without 
Parties epoch shows a partial regression (HR = 0.803, $p$ = 0.025 
in Model~3), significantly better than State Formation but 
substantially worse than the power-sharing and transition epochs.

A likelihood ratio test confirms that epochs explain far more 
variation in ministerial survival than portfolio membership alone. 
Adding epoch to a portfolio-only model improves fit by LRT = 73.46 
on 4 degrees of freedom ($p$ $<$ 0.001), while adding portfolio to 
an epoch-only model produces a more modest improvement of 
LRT = 10.69 on 4 degrees of freedom ($p$ = 0.030). The 
institutional period in which a minister serves matters more for 
survival than the portfolio they hold.

Taken together, these results suggest a partial and non-monotonic 
answer to the first research question. Peru's critical junctures 
did not uniformly reproduce the same continuity conditions across 
two centuries. The transition from the Oligarchic State to the 
Power-Sharing State produced a genuine and durable improvement --- 
the largest single shift detectable in the data. But the democratic 
epoch that followed Fujimori's constitutional refoundation failed 
to sustain those conditions. The continuity conditions of the most 
recent epoch revert toward the founding period rather than the 
mid-century peak. This pattern is broadly consistent with the 
near-miss argument: successive critical junctures altered the 
formal institutional landscape without consistently transforming 
the informal mechanisms through which political instability is 
managed.

\subsection{Do Some Portfolios Systematically Escape the 
Dominant Pattern?}

The second research question asks whether some sectors of the 
state systematically escape the dominant continuity logic of their 
epoch, exhibiting duration patterns consistent with the presence 
of a protective belt. The epoch analysis established that the 
institutional period matters more for ministerial survival than 
any individual characteristic. The question now is whether, within 
that institutional logic, some portfolios consistently deviate from 
what the epoch-level pattern would predict.

research question. Some portfolios do escape the dominant 
continuity logic of their epoch --- but not in the way that a 
simple portfolio ranking would suggest. The protective belt is not 
an inherent feature of the Economy ministry. It is a historically 
constructed cost structure that emerged after 1993 when external 
accountability relationships --- international creditors, fiscal 
rules, sovereign debt markets --- raised the political price of 
Economy minister replacement beyond what domestic pressure alone 
could sustain. Before that consolidation, Economy ministers were 
as expendable as any other. After it, they were not. The 
instability itself remained; what changed was which portfolios 
the escape valve could reach.

\section{Discussion}

The empirical results presented above invite interpretation at two 
levels: what they reveal about Peru specifically, and what they 
suggest about the relationship between institutional change, 
continuity, and organizational learning more broadly.

\subsection{Institutional Change Without Institutional Learning}

The central finding of this study is that Peru's critical junctures 
did not produce monotonic improvements in the conditions governing 
ministerial continuity. Two centuries of republican history, five 
institutional epochs, and 1,062 ministerial spells later, the most 
recent democratic period generates continuity conditions closer to 
the founding epoch than to the mid-century peak. This is not a 
story of gradual institutional development interrupted by occasional 
setbacks. It is a story of structural reproduction interrupted by 
one genuine improvement --- the Power-Sharing State --- that 
subsequent critical junctures failed to sustain.

The distinction between institutional change and institutional 
learning, introduced in the theoretical framework, finds its 
clearest empirical expression here. Peru has changed its 
institutions repeatedly --- thirteen constitutions, multiple regime 
transitions, democratic openings and authoritarian closures. Yet 
the continuity conditions under which ministerial experience 
accumulates have not improved in any consistent direction. The 
Oligarchic State, despite representing a period of formal 
reconstruction after the humiliation of the War of the Pacific, 
produced median ministerial durations almost identical to the 
founding epoch. The constitutional refoundation of 1993, despite 
introducing macroeconomic stabilization and a new institutional 
framework, failed to sustain the continuity conditions that the 
Power-Sharing and Transition epochs had produced. Formal 
institutional change and improvement in learning conditions have 
repeatedly failed to coincide.

This pattern is precisely what the near-miss framework predicts. 
Each major rupture --- the War of the Pacific, the Crisis of the 
Aristocratic Republic, the Military Revolution, Fujimori's 
self-coup --- represented a moment of genuine contingency in which 
alternative institutional trajectories were plausible. In each 
case, the informal mechanism through which political pressure is 
absorbed --- ministerial dismissal as the escape valve --- survived 
the juncture and was reproduced in the subsequent path. The 
formal rules changed. The informal institution did not.

\subsection{The Instability Itself Is the Institution}

The most theoretically significant finding is not the variation 
across epochs but the mechanism that explains the reproduction of 
poor continuity conditions across them. The escape valve is not 
simply a symptom of weak institutions. It is itself an institution 
--- informal, durable, and self-reinforcing. Each time a minister 
is sacrificed to relieve political pressure, the expectation that 
dismissal is the appropriate response to crisis is reinforced. 
The political cost of \emph{not} activating the escape valve rises. 
The informal institution deepens its lock-in.

This finding flips the conventional interpretation of Peruvian 
political instability. Most accounts treat chronic ministerial 
turnover as evidence of institutional weakness --- the absence of 
stable rules, norms, and enforcement mechanisms. The present 
analysis suggests the opposite: the turnover is stable precisely 
because it is institutionalized. What persists across two centuries 
is not the absence of institutions but the presence of an informal 
one whose purpose is to absorb political pressure without requiring 
structural change.

Tomasi di Lampedusa captured the logic before political science 
named it: \textit{``Se vogliamo che tutto rimanga com'è, bisogna 
che tutto cambi''} --- if we want everything to remain as it is, 
everything must change. Peru's political class has learned this 
lesson well. Governments change. Cabinets change. Constitutions 
change. The repertoire through which instability is managed 
remains largely intact. The minister is Tancredi's gesture: a 
sacrifice offered so that the system does not have to transform 
itself.

\subsection{The Protective Belt as Proof of the Conditional}

The protective belt finding does more than identify an exception 
to the dominant pattern. It provides the critical theoretical test 
that distinguishes between two interpretations of Peru's chronic 
instability.

The first interpretation holds that instability is culturally or 
structurally inevitable --- an expression of deep social 
fragmentation, weak civic capacity, and the absence of the 
rationality preconditions that would allow competence-based 
ministerial evaluation. On this reading, Peru cannot produce 
sustained ministerial continuity because the conditions for doing 
so do not exist.

The second interpretation holds that instability is institutionally 
produced --- the outcome of a specific cost structure in which 
dismissal is cheap and continuity is not rewarded. On this reading, 
Peru can produce sustained continuity wherever the cost structure 
changes, and the escape valve is not a cultural inevitability but 
a political equilibrium that can be disrupted by external forces.

The Economy portfolio in the Democracy without Parties epoch 
supports the second interpretation. When international creditors, 
sovereign debt markets, fiscal rules, and multilateral oversight 
raised the cost of Economy minister replacement beyond what 
domestic political pressure could sustain, continuity improved --- 
not because Peruvian political culture changed, not because 
presidents became more committed to institutional stability, but 
because the incentive structure changed. The belt is not a 
cultural achievement. It is a cost structure imposed from outside.

This finding has implications beyond Peru. It suggests that 
protective belts --- localized continuity-preserving mechanisms 
generated by external accountability --- may operate in any 
executive branch where certain portfolios are embedded in 
international financial or diplomatic networks. The mechanism is 
not specific to the Peruvian case. What is specific to Peru is 
the contrast between the narrow sector where the belt operates 
and the vast majority of the state where it does not.

\subsection{What the Democratic Epoch Reveals}

The partial regression of continuity conditions in the Democracy 
without Parties epoch deserves specific attention. The data show 
that the most recent democratic period --- characterized by 
universal suffrage, competitive elections, constitutional 
continuity, and macroeconomic stability --- produced worse 
continuity conditions than the military and power-sharing periods 
that preceded it. This finding will appear paradoxical to readers 
who expect democracy to improve institutional quality.

The paradox dissolves once the escape valve logic is applied. 
Democratic accountability in a system without programmatic parties 
creates strong incentives for ministerial dismissal. Presidents 
facing fragmented congresses, high electoral volatility, and 
personalist political competition cannot build stable coalitions 
through policy commitments. What they can do is demonstrate 
responsiveness --- and the most visible form of responsiveness 
available is the ministerial reshuffle. Democracy without parties 
does not weaken the escape valve. It intensifies it, because the 
political cost of \emph{not} reshuffling in response to pressure 
is higher when electoral survival depends on visible performance 
signals rather than on party loyalty.

The 11.4 percent of spells ending within thirty days in the 
democratic epoch --- nearly triple the rate of the Power-Sharing 
epoch --- is the quantitative signature of this dynamic. Ministers 
are appointed and removed before they can structure the problems 
they were appointed to address, let alone convert their experience 
into institutional knowledge. The knowledge spiral cannot start. 
The organized anarchy reproduces itself. And the next crisis finds 
the same ill-structured problems waiting, with the same absence 
of accumulated knowledge, for the next minister to begin again 
from zero.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper began with a question that two centuries of Peruvian 
republican history make unavoidable: why has a political system 
that has changed so much --- constitutions, regimes, coalitions, 
economic models --- changed so little in the way it manages the 
people responsible for governing? The answer developed here is 
not that Peru lacks institutions. It is that Peru has an 
institution --- the escape valve --- whose function is precisely 
to absorb the pressure for change without producing it.

The analysis rests on an original dataset of 1,062 ministerial 
spells covering five portfolios from 1821 to 2026, organized into 
five institutional epochs defined by the critical junctures that 
periodically restructured Peru's political logic. Ministerial 
duration is treated not as an outcome to be explained but as a 
signal --- an observable trace of the continuity conditions under 
which governing experience either accumulates into institutional 
knowledge or is repeatedly lost. The central empirical question 
is whether Peru's critical junctures ever produced durable 
improvements in those conditions. The answer is partial: one 
juncture did, most did not, and the most recent democratic epoch 
represents a partial regression toward the founding period's 
pattern of chronic interruption.

Four conclusions follow from the analysis.

\textbf{First}, institutional epochs explain ministerial survival 
far better than portfolio membership. The institutional period in 
which a minister serves --- not their individual traits, not the 
portfolio they hold --- is the primary determinant of how long 
they remain in office. This finding situates the present analysis 
within the ministerial survival literature while departing from 
its standard assumptions: rather than asking what traits or 
contextual factors predict individual duration, it asks what 
patterns of duration reveal about the institutional environment 
that produces them.

\textbf{Second}, Peru's critical junctures did not uniformly 
improve continuity conditions. The largest and most durable shift 
in the data --- a reduction of roughly fifty percent in exit 
hazard --- occurred in the transition from the Oligarchic State 
to the Power-Sharing State. The Transition epoch sustained those 
conditions. The democratic epoch that followed Fujimori's 
constitutional refoundation did not. Formal institutional change 
and improvement in learning conditions have repeatedly failed 
to coincide, consistent with the near-miss argument developed 
in the theoretical framework.

\textbf{Third}, the instability itself is the institution. What 
has been reproduced across two centuries is not the absence of 
stable rules but the presence of an informal one: the expectation 
that ministerial dismissal is the appropriate response to political 
crisis, regardless of the minister's competence or the structural 
nature of the problem. This informal escape valve is self-reinforcing 
--- each activation raises the political cost of non-activation 
and deepens the lock-in of the pattern. Path dependence in Peru 
operates primarily at the informal level while the formal 
institutional landscape churns.

\textbf{Fourth}, the protective belt is real but historically 
specific. The Economy portfolio gained a significant survival 
advantage only in the Democracy without Parties epoch, when 
external accountability structures --- international creditors, 
fiscal rules, sovereign debt markets, multilateral oversight --- 
raised the cost of Economy minister replacement beyond what 
domestic political pressure alone could sustain. In all earlier 
epochs, Economy ministers faced the same exit hazard as Interior 
ministers. The belt was not inherited from the portfolio's 
institutional position. It was constructed by a specific 
historical configuration of external constraints. And crucially, 
it coexisted with the broader escape valve logic operating 
everywhere else in the cabinet. The state did not escape the 
learning trap. One corner of it did.

\subsection{Contributions and Limitations}

This paper makes three contributions to the existing literature. 
The first is methodological: it introduces the institutional 
epoch as the primary unit of historical comparison in ministerial 
survival analysis, replacing the standard practice of pooling 
spells across history or controlling for time as a continuous 
covariate. The epoch framework allows survival analysis to detect 
whether critical junctures produced durable changes in continuity 
conditions --- a question the existing literature has not asked.

The second contribution is theoretical: it connects the 
ministerial survival literature to organizational learning theory 
through the concept of knowledge accumulation. Survival time is 
reframed not as an individual outcome but as a systemic signal. 
This reframing generates the protective belt concept as a natural 
corollary: wherever external accountability raises the cost of 
continuity interruption, a localized space for knowledge 
accumulation emerges within a broader environment of institutional 
instability.

The third contribution is empirical: two centuries of ministerial 
duration data for Peru, organized into a dataset that did not 
previously exist and that extends the Latin American ministerial 
survival literature into the nineteenth century and into a case 
that the comparative literature has largely treated as too 
unstable for systematic analysis.

The analysis has limitations that define the agenda for future 
work. The most fundamental is that organizational learning is not 
directly observed. Ministerial duration is a proxy for the 
continuity conditions from which learning may emerge, not a 
measure of learning itself. Demonstrating that longer tenure 
actually produced better policy outcomes, more effective 
bureaucracies, or improved state capacity would require 
ministry-level performance data that are not currently available 
for the full historical period. Future work could extend the 
analysis to include time-varying indicators of political pressure 
--- protests, censure motions, approval ratings, executive-legislative 
conflict --- to test the escape valve mechanism more directly. 
It could also extend the protective belt argument to other 
portfolios and other countries where external accountability 
structures vary, generating comparative leverage on the 
conditions under which belts form and dissolve.

\subsection{A Final Observation}

The France--Peru comparison with which this paper opened has a 
resolution that the data make visible. France broke its path 
at a critical juncture that produced not merely new institutions 
but new continuity conditions --- a durable shift in how long 
premiers serve and therefore in how much governing experience 
can accumulate. Peru has had the junctures without the shift. 
The disruption nexus arrived repeatedly. The ideas lying around 
were the same ones that had failed before. And the escape valve 
absorbed the pressure each time, ensuring that the system could 
change everything --- governments, cabinets, constitutions --- 
so that the informal institution at its core could remain 
exactly as it was.

Whether Peru's sixth critical juncture, still unfolding as this 
paper goes to press, will produce a different outcome remains 
genuinely open. The data cannot answer that question. What they 
can say is that the conditions for a different answer have never 
been more visible --- or more contested.
